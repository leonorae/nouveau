Process Notes

These poems were generated by building a system of composable text transformations and running gathered source material through them. No neural model was involved in the selection — the outputs shown here are the results of purely algorithmic operations (shuffling, erasure, dictionary displacement, Markov chains, interleaving) applied to human-written poetry.

The most surprising discovery was how much the source text matters more than the technique. Erasure on Strand ("Keeping Things Whole") produces something that feels like a Strand poem because his vocabulary is already so precise that any subset carries weight. Erasure on lesser material just produces fragments. The same N+7 offset applied to Dickinson vs. Szymborska produces wildly different textures because their syntactic skeletons are so different.

The soft constraint approach — scoring candidates on a gradient rather than filtering them through a gate — turned out to be the most important architectural decision. A hard lipogram that bans the letter 'e' produces Szymborska's "I word / first to past / I word / I it" — striking but brutal. A soft lipogram that merely prefers candidates with fewer e's produces text that leans away from 'e' without collapsing into telegraph. The soft version is more like a sensibility than a rule.

The composed aesthetic profiles (sparse, dense, ghost, echo, strange) are the most interesting outcome. Each one is just a weighted sum of simple scorers, but they genuinely prefer different text. "rust knows what iron forgot" scores perfectly under ghost (no 'e', terse, novel) and terribly under dense (too short, not alliterative). The profiles behave like different readers with different tastes, which is exactly what you want for multi-agent scenarios: each agent has a reward function that encodes an aesthetic.

The poems I keep returning to are the ones where the technique reveals something the original concealed. Strand erased to "am ... what ... I ... part the ... body has been / We all have reasons ... move to ..." strips his poem to its existential armature. Simic's stone becoming a tower, then half-erased to "I am light to ... a tower / ... must ... ember ... / shield seed wave is ... psalm" — the displacement finds a religious register that was latent in the original's imagery of inner walls and star-charts.

The Markov outputs are the wildest. "stone I destroy it must be cool and always the word Nothing" fuses Simic's stone with Szymborska's philosophical grammar into something neither poet wrote but both would recognize. The chain doesn't understand either poem, but it finds the seam where they touch.

All of this is infrastructure for the real thing: a human and an AI writing together, with these transformations and constraints shaping what the AI produces. The scorer architecture is already the reward function for RL. The context transformers are already the prompt engineering. What's missing is the loop — the model generating, the human responding, the model learning what this particular human finds beautiful. That's next.
